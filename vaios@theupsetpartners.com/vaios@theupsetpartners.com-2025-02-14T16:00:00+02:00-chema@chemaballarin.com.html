Qualification Questions +

  1. What stage is your startup at currently, and what are your primary growth challenges in terms of customer acquisition and market expansion?

    • This helps understand if they're a good fit for the service and their specific needs

  2. What is your current go-to-market strategy, and how are you currently acquiring customers?

    • This reveals if they're relying on personal networks/word-of-mouth and need a more systematic approach

  3. What are your growth targets for the next 6-12 months, and what markets are you looking to expand into?

    • This helps assess urgency and scope of potential engagement

  4. What is your current sales infrastructure and team composition? Do you have dedicated sales resources?

    • This determines which service tier might be most appropriate (from Search to Scale)

  5. What is your average deal size and sales cycle length currently?

    • This helps evaluate if they fit the ideal profile of mid-market targeting with balanced sales velocity and revenue per account

These questions align with the company's framework (4S - Search, Service, Sales, Scale) and help determine if the prospect has the challenges their service solves (lack of systematic customer acquisition, reliance on personal networks, need for scalable growth systems).

Objection Handling +

Based on the context and adding my expertise as a sales researcher, here are the most relevant arguments to overcome these objections:

1. Budget/Price concerns:

  • Focus on ROI and value demonstration rather than cost
  • Break down the investment into smaller, more manageable payment terms
  • Show case studies/examples of how similar customers achieved positive returns
  • Highlight the cost of NOT implementing the solution (opportunity cost)

2. Long decision-making process/Multiple stakeholders:

  • Map out all stakeholders early and understand their individual concerns
  • Provide educational content tailored to each stakeholder's role and priorities
  • Create a clear timeline with milestones for the decision process
  • Offer pilot programs or proof of concept to reduce perceived risk
  • Build internal champions who can help navigate the approval process
  • Provide materials that stakeholders can use to "sell" internally

  • Understanding and mapping complex customer journeys

The key is to be patient while actively managing the process and providing value at each step of the decision-making journey.
